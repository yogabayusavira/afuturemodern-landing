# AFuture Modern Knowledge Base

## Executive Summary

AFuture Modern (also called Future Modern) is a **member-owned cooperative network** for creative talent and service clients. It explicitly organizes around a *Venture Labor* model: workers share ownership (through equity and tokens) of the value they create. According to internal documents, the company’s mission is to **“capture surplus value” and give it to those who built it**. 

It serves artists, freelancers, and companies by offering managed services, talent networks, and platform tools in a cooperative structure. Unlike traditional gig marketplaces, AFuture Modern emphasizes *community ownership*, token-based incentives, and equity “earned, not bought” by contributors. 

Its tagline – “Workers built it. Capital kept it.” – summarizes the perceived problem of wealth extraction from labor, and AFuture Modern’s aim to reverse that. Key facts include: it has a 1,000+ person talent network, issues a token ($BUILD) where 95% is shared with members, and follows core values of **Provenance, Discernment, and Equity**. Major open questions include how it will scale and exactly how the cooperative governance works in practice. AFuture Modern uses a Venture Labor OS that attributes revenue to the people or collective that created the paid outcome, rather than treating all revenue as company-owned by default. Its standard waterfall allocates 85% to direct labor, 12% to active administration, and 3% to treasury and liquidity.

AFuture Modern views this mismatch as a problem to solve by curating a high-quality network.

* **Cultural and Creative Stagnation:** Some mission statements imply a problem with how culture and creation are commercialized. Phrases like “revolutionize the culture of creation” hint at a belief that traditional models stifle creativity (though this is less detailed in the docs).

## Cooperative Structure and Venture Labor OS

AFuture Modern’s core economic model is the Venture Labor OS: a cooperative system designed to make the person, team, or collective that creates a paid outcome visible and paid accordingly. Its central rule is that every transaction has attributable labor behind it. That labor may be client delivery, closing a deal, sourcing a partnership, generating a lead, managing an account, building a relationship, placing a link, setting up an account, making an introduction, or deploying cooperative capital. Where attribution is clear, the contributor is credited. Where no individual contributor can be identified, the documents treat AFuture Modern’s collective infrastructure, brand, platform, or capital as the contributor. The model explicitly rejects a “100% House with no service provider” classification. 

## Revenue Allocation and Payment Waterfall

The standard revenue waterfall allocates 85% of gross revenue to the service-provider pool, 12% to active administration, and 3% to treasury. Treasury is split evenly: 1.5% for operations and 1.5% for the liquidity pool. The 85% service-provider allocation applies to whoever created the client-paid deliverable, including a commission-only closer, a lead generator and account manager, or a member sourcing a brand partnership, sponsorship, affiliate relationship, grant, or channel deal. The 12% pool is for active administrative work such as coordination, invoicing, and management when that work did not itself create the client deliverable. 

## Direct Labor versus Administration

AFuture Modern classifies work by the value created for the client, not by the worker’s payment structure. A person paid by salary can qualify for the service-provider pool if they create the paid deliverable, while a commission-only salesperson can qualify as direct labor when they source, develop, manage, and close the relationship that produces the deal. Administrative and support work remains in the admin pool when it supports the operation but does not itself create the client-paid deliverable. 

## Opportunity Origination and Ownership

The documents distinguish between a Company Opportunity and a member’s independent opportunity. A Company Opportunity is one that arose through AFuture Modern’s client relationships, infrastructure, accounts, platforms, brand, reputation, resources, or a member acting as an AFuture Modern representative. In those cases, AFuture Modern has its standard house entitlement: 15% of gross revenue, represented by the 12% active-admin allocation and 3% treasury allocation. Work that originates entirely through a member’s personal network, without AFuture Modern involvement and in a non-competing capacity, is described as the member’s independent income rather than company revenue. 

## Member-Originated Work Brought Into AFuture Modern

When a member independently originates a relationship or opportunity and voluntarily brings AFuture Modern in for fulfillment, AFuture Modern’s stated standard entitlement is 15% on the work that flows through its fulfillment structure. The remaining relationship premium belongs to the originating member. Any amount directed to AFuture Modern above its standard 15% is characterized as voluntary generosity, not a precedent or future obligation. 

## Treasury, Liquidity, and $BUILD

The Venture Labor OS includes a treasury and liquidity layer intended to convert a portion of operating revenue into shared cooperative capacity. The treasury receives 3% of gross revenue, with half designated for operations and half directed toward a liquidity pool. The diagrams label that liquidity pool as $BUILD token, describing it as a mechanism for passive equity accrual and potential OTC mobilization. The precise legal structure, token mechanics, ownership rights, valuation method, transferability, regulatory treatment, and implementation status of $BUILD are not established in the provided documents.

## Risks, Gaps, and Unclear Areas

Several **risks and unknowns** emerge from the documents:

* **Scalability of Cooperative Governance:** Managing 1,000+ members democratically can be challenging. The documents do not explain how decisions scale or how disputes are resolved. Risk: bureaucracy or slow decision-making.

* **Token/Economic Risks:** The platform heavily relies on its token economy. Fluctuations in token value, regulatory issues (security laws), or technical faults could harm the model. The docs don’t address these contingencies.

* **Client Acquisition and Revenue:** While talent is a focus, it’s unclear how many paying clients AFM has or how it markets services to them. The Alibaba pitch targets large partners, but we lack concrete client pipeline info. Risk: reliance on community contributions without stable revenue sources.

* **Member Incentives vs. Free Riding:** “Earned membership” is a value, but does the coop prevent members from just taking rewards without fully contributing? The Code emphasizes earning, but details of enforcement aren’t clear.

* **Competition:** If AFM’s idea proves successful, traditional gig platforms could adapt or new cooperatives could form. The docs don’t discuss competitive strategy.

* **Product Focus:** The mention of XR and AR products is intriguing, but it’s a niche market. It’s unclear if this distracts from or complements the core talent marketplace. Risk: resource diversion.

* **Missing Operational Details:** Many specifics are simply **not established**. For example, the exact workflow of hiring a member for a project, how disputes are handled, pricing of services, or how clients pay (cash vs. crypto) are not described. This means adopting the model could have hidden challenges.

There were no glaring contradictions in the materials (the vision and the mechanics align). However, some ambition-versus-execution gaps appear: the bold statements about equality vs. the real-world numbers (the company is still small). Thus, the **overall risk** is whether the project can live up to its ideals and scale without stumbling on the practical gaps not covered in the docs.

## Additional Venture Labor Terms

• Service Provider (SP): The individual, team, or cooperative entity that created the client-paid deliverable. The standard allocation is 85% of gross revenue. 

• Active Admin: Members performing coordination, invoicing, management, or other support work that does not itself create the client-paid deliverable. The standard allocation is 12% of gross revenue. 

• Company Opportunity: A deal that arose through AFuture Modern’s client relationships, infrastructure, accounts, platform, brand, resources, or a member acting as an AFuture Modern representative. 

• Member-Originated Opportunity: A deal originating from a member’s independent network without AFuture Modern involvement. It remains the member’s unless they voluntarily bring AFuture Modern into fulfillment. 

• Relationship Premium: Value above AFuture Modern’s standard fulfillment entitlement that comes from a member’s personal relationship or negotiating leverage. Under the commission matrix, this belongs to the originating member. 

• Treasury: The 3% portion of gross revenue allocated to cooperative operations and liquidity, split 1.5% for operations and 1.5% for the liquidity pool.
